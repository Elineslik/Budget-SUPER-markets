Moa Aolank

Online

  • Pronouns: she / her
  • Age: 22 years old
  • Orign: Finland
  • Study: Journalism
  • Minor: International Journalism at AUAS

‘I am studying Journalism and Communication, a bachelor’s programme at the University of Helsinki. Usually, journalism is very practical, but because it’s a university degree there is a strong focus on research. My studies are in Swedish, because I belong to the Swedish speaking minority in Finland. At home we speak both Finnish and Swedish, so I speak both languages equally well.

am currently doing a minor programme in International Journalism at the AUAS. It is much more practical than what I am used to. We have to write so many articles, which is good because it has helped me improve my English so much, having to write in English almost every day and talking too.

visited Amsterdam for the first time in 2015, and I enjoyed it a lot. I heard from others that it’s nice to study here. At first, I was a bit hesitant, because the application process is a lot of work. But then I started researching and the idea grew into a decision that I am incredibly happy with. I have always been interested in going abroad. When I was in ninth grade, I went to Germany for one month. I also worked abroad for a few summers in Denmark. You can build your own identity again when living in other countries.’

The Amsterdam way

‘I live in Diemen, but I wish I lived closer to the city centre. I cycle everywhere because I want to do it the Amsterdam way. I like how easy it is to get around by bike, I am not used to that. At first, I was a bit afraid of the traffic, but if you are careful enough it is fine. The Dutch language sounds made-up or as if someone is talking backwards, I am sorry Dutchies but it does. The supermarkets in my opinion are cheap, because Finland is extremely expensive. The people here are very direct, but I am also used to being direct. Most other students in my class are open, so it is easy to get to know people.’

"I cycle everywhere, because I want to do it the Amsterdam way."

The international lifestyle

'I enjoy the night life. There is always something happening somewhere. My favourite place to go is Skatecafé. At the beginning of the semester I changed my minor, so it took me a while to get to know the people in my class, but this was the first time I hung out with them. We had a pre-party and then we went out to the Skatecafé., which was a lot of fun.

About budgeting tips, I would say: make your food at home. Also, go cycling. Renting a Swapfiets doesn’t cost that much, and you get free exercise and a moment to clear your mind after a long day. Another tip is that you must remind yourself that you are going to have bad days and good days. Also, just say yes to everything. You are going to meet many people that way.

I'm graduating after this and this is the first time in my life that I do not have a plan, which is freeing and scary. I either want to go travelling or backpacking in Asia or move to Sweden and work there. Studying something creative or moving to Germany and learning the language properly is on my list too. In the end, we will see what I will do.’

Fernando Nagore Piedrahita

Offline

  • Pronouns: he / him
  • Age: 22 years old
  • Orign: Spain
  • Study: International Business in Berlin
  • Minor: International Economics and Globalization at AUAS

‘I am from Berlin, where I study International Business. I have quite a lot of international experience. Before my exchange semester in Amsterdam, I also did one in Mexico which took six months. Also, I’ve done an internship in Luxembourg. I’ve always had the desire to study abroad. Whenever I get the chance, I pack my stuff and leave. Now I’m not following a specific minor programme. My specialisation is marketing, so I’m taking some economic and business classes.

I moved to Amsterdam at the end of January, so I’ve been here a solid three months now. I’ve been to Amsterdam before though, during an Interrail trip that is popular among the Berlin students. I spent four days in Amsterdam and liked it a lot. After studying in Mexico, I wanted to go back to Europe because I wanted to be closer to my family. I chose Amsterdam as my next study destination because it is in the heart of Europe, it’s easy to travel to many countries from here.’

Diemen, the place to be

‘At the moment I live in a big student complex in Diemen. A lot of other international students live here. It wasn’t my first choice, but I understand the current housing situation in Amsterdam is not the best. The positive side of living in a complex like this is you can relate to the other internationals because you’re all in the same boat. It’s also cheaper than living in the city centre. However, it could take a bit away from the whole experience of surrounding yourself with the Dutch population.’

"First things first: get yourself a bike! Also, buy your own drinks and organise your own parties."

Get that bike!

‘One of the first things I noticed in Amsterdam is how expensive everything is. Especially public transport. It is well organised, but as an international student who isn’t staying very long, there is no discount card. I would highly recommend getting yourself a bike to get around the city cheap and fast. Amsterdam is not that huge, so it’s easy to get around. Even on rainy days, just accept it and jump on your bike, just like the Dutch do.’

The Dutch know their English well

‘Another thing I discovered is that Dutch people are very good at English. Out of all the countries I’ve visited, the Dutch are by far the best English-speaking population. Even the older generations directly switch to English if they discover you don’t speak Dutch. I sometimes find it easier to understand Dutch people speaking English than English people themselves. When I visited Manchester in the UK, the accents were sometimes very varied and hard to understand.’

Study hotspots

‘I would really recommend CREA on the Nieuwe Achtergracht. It’s a nice, inspiring place to study at. A lot of my classmates also like the OBA, the big library near Central Station. Apart from that I just really like hanging out in the city centre, I think it’s beautiful and it’s fun to give tours to my friends who visit regularly.’

Jiyoung Lee

Online

  • Pronouns: she / her
  • Age: 22 years old
  • Orign: South Korea
  • Study: Fashion Branding in Amsterdam
  • Minor: Fashion & Visual Culture at AUAS

I have always been fascinated with studying abroad and the choice to start studying in Amsterdam came to mind in 2017. It was my mum who came up with the idea of studying here because she read online that Amsterdam is a welcoming city for internationals and has affordable study programmes. There was no connection to The Netherlands before that and it was only in that moment that this small country suddenly appeared as an option.

choice to study abroad was influenced by my parents. My mum has one big rule in her life; she wanted her daughter to learn the English language, even though she couldn’t speak it herself. She wanted her daughter to learn to make my world the biggest it could be and this really encouraged me to see no boundaries in where I could go.

Recently, I moved to the city centre with a friend, there is a heavy rent with this so I also started working. I am sharing the house together with three different people, nationalities and circumstances and it makes me feel that my world is big. When I need it I can get some alone time, which is also important to reload and get my own routine going.’

Comparisons and expectations

‘Compared to my experience in Korea, where all the buildings are tall and grey, the city of Amsterdam almost felt like a maze. As a fashion student, I was impressed by all the different outfits from pedestrians and tourists. From a Korean perspective that is not a normal thing to see. Everybody wears what they want to, and people are okay with it, something I really applaud as a fashion student. It was quite shocking that the Dutch people were nice but at the same time direct. Through my study, I realised that Dutch students have more optimistic viewpoints on the process of work. They are way more open about their struggles, they will keep on searching for a solution.

I thought the city was so beautiful and romantic, that I could call it my home. But I really started to feel at home when I started working and mixing with Dutch people. When you go up to them and even try to speak the language, they are always open to help. They also express mutual interest, even if it is in a small way like asking me ‘what are you studying’ it makes me feel so welcome.

"I already feel so welcome in Amsterdam."

Biking is the ultimate Dutch thing for sure. But what I think is super Dutch is the self-scan system at the supermarket. I couldn’t believe that they just let people scan stuff by themselves and then walk out. The Dutch also have a lot of fried food, the sweet potatoes with truffle mayonnaise are definitely my favourite. But honestly, Amsterdam’s food is so varied, and every culture is represented and I think that is an iconic character of the Dutch cuisine.’

Budget tips

‘My budget tip is selling vintage clothes as well as buying them. Amsterdam has a lot of second-hand stores and flea markets where you can find nice things. Also, try to cook at home sometimes, it is way cheaper, and you can save small portions for later. And my tip for any international student is to just take your time with speaking English and don’t hesitate to ask for some clarification when necessary. People don’t mind.

After my study, I am open to exploring more of the world or even studying somewhere else abroad as well. But when I think of Amsterdam, this could definitely be a place I would come back to and grow old. Changing the city you live in makes you grow so much as a person and I am open to explore more.’

  • Minor: Global Trendwatching at AUAS

‘This semester I’m enrolled in the minor programme Global Trendwatching at the Amsterdam University of Applied Sciences and before I studied International Journalism, also at AUAS. In France, I study Information and Communication at Université Paris Cité. It was always in my mind to go abroad one day. However, I didn’t have a specific city in my mind. But when I went to Amsterdam, I immediately got attracted by the big European capital with its bike culture, the canals, the greenness, and it was a great opportunity to improve my English too.’

‘I immediately fell in love with the city.’

The Amsterdam Experience

‘My first impression of Amsterdam was so good. I immediately fell in love with the city, mostly because of the bike culture. Cycling is a feeling of freedom for me. Especially here, as I’m far away from my family and friends and everything that is familiar to me. I do live with other international students, in Diemen, which makes the studying abroad experience awesome. I found the place with the help of my university, so I didn't have to struggle at all. Living in this enormous city gave me this favourite memory of me and two other international guys. We would spend a lot of time sitting next to the canal at our favourite spot near the NEMO Science Museum listening to music, having beers, dancing, singing, laughing, and playing football. Those moments summarise my experience of studying in Amsterdam. It is interesting to think about how I didn't know anything about Amsterdam when I arrived. Now that I know a lot of people, I can say that the city feels familiar to me.’

vs French Culture

‘When it comes to the Dutch culture, several things stood out to me, other than the fact that the people are tall. One of them is that here, you call the lecturers by their first names. In France you would never do that. Instead, we call them sir or madam. The second one, which also happens at university, is that you can go to the bathroom whenever you want to without asking for permission. In France you would have to raise your hand, otherwise it can come off as rude. Just like eating snacks in class, in the Netherlands, it's completely normal which feels odd to me. What I have noticed is that the relationship between students and teachers is based on trust, which I do like. And the last one is that during New Year's Eve, I noticed that Dutch people rather do cool drinking games instead of dancing during parties.’

Budget & Café Tips

‘For any new international student who decides to study in Amsterdam, I would recommend getting a bonus card from Albert Heijn. You can save a lot of money using that card to buy groceries. Febo is a nice and easy place to grab food, especially to grab a broodje kroket with mustard. For bikes, check out Swapfiets as they are good too. To hang out with friends I would suggest visiting Skatecafé in North and Coco’s Outback at Thorbeckeplein to watch football while having drinks. And if you have time left, make sure to organise a boat tour on the canal with friends! I would love to live a part of my life in Amsterdam. My friend and I agreed on coming back to Amsterdam to spend one week together. I would still love to see an Ajax game, but it is so hard and expensive to get tickets.’
